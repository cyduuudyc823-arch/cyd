01 — Problem Framing

Context

Rapid growth in pet ownership in China has increased demand for veterinary blood transfusions, yet structured donation systems remain limited. Resources are fragmented, blood type matching is complex, and unregulated markets introduce safety and ethical risks — making emergency donor searches highly stressful for pet owners.

Design Challenge

How might we:
Reduce emergency stress for pet owners?
Build sustainable donor networks instead of one-off matching?
02 — Research & Discovery

Methods

  • — Interviews with pet owners
  • — Veterinary workflow observation
  • — Secondary research

Key Findings

Owners experience panic when searching for donors during emergencies.

Trust and familiarity increase willingness to donate.

Existing systems focus on transactional matching rather than relationship building.

FIELD WORK 01
03 — Key Insights

Insight 01

Emergency-only systems create emotional pressure.

Insight 02 — Two-way Matching

Two-way matching increases trust and participation.

Key elements:
  • • Same blood type compatibility
  • • Geographic proximity
  • • Ongoing relationship building
Shift: Reactive → Relationship-based

Insight 03

Community networks reduce risk during emergencies.

STRATEGY
⭐ 04 — Behavioural Design Strategy

Friendship-based Donor Network

Instead of viewing blood donation as a medical transaction, the system reframes participation as relationship-building.

PRINCIPLE 01

Familiarity reduces hesitation.

PRINCIPLE 02

Social reciprocity increases willingness to help.

PRINCIPLE 03

Peer connection transforms obligation into community support.

Key Mechanism

Cats with compatible blood types and nearby locations form “friend networks.” Owners can build ongoing relationships through daily interaction. Emergency support becomes a by-product of existing social ties.

09 — Reflection & Impact
REF 01

Reflection 01: Quantifying Social Impact

Design is not only about pixels, but about measurable outcomes.

If implemented in real-world operations, I would establish Trust Metrics to evaluate impact. For example, analysing the correlation between community interaction frequency and emergency response time could help validate whether social relationships effectively reduce blood-matching search duration.

REF 02

Reflection 02: Balancing Privacy and Accessibility

Building a community-based network raises important questions about balancing geographic visibility with owner privacy. Moving forward, I would explore privacy-first UX approaches, such as approximate location sharing or layered information disclosure, to maintain efficiency while ensuring safe boundaries.

REF 03

Reflection 03: Future Scalability

This model demonstrates strong scalability and transferability. It could expand from cat donor networks to rare dog breeds or even rare disease communities. Through this project, I realised that the core of service design is not solving isolated pain points, but reshaping collaborative ecosystems.

01 — Problem Framing: The “Silent” Majority

Context

China’s urbanisation rate has reached 64%, yet community planning remains largely driven by top-down decision-making structures. Despite policy efforts to encourage participation, residents often feel disconnected from shaping their local futures.

URBANIZATION DATA VIZ
Design Challenge

How might we enable meaningful participation in community futures?

02 — Research & Discovery

Methods
  • — Rapid exploratory interviews
  • — Stakeholder interviews
  • — Desk research
Observed Pain Points

Cognitive Gap: Residents struggle to imagine future scenarios; discussions rely on abstract terminology.

Social Pressure: Offline meetings are influenced by dominant voices or small groups.

Participation Apathy: Residents feel their input does not lead to real change.

INSIGHTS

03 — Key Insights

Imagination Needs Tangible Anchors

Abstract future discussions fail because residents lack concrete entry points.

Design implication: translate technology into everyday life scenarios.

Participation Requires Psychological Safety

Social dynamics reduce honest expression.

Design implication: introduce anonymous and structured interaction.

Engagement Emerges Through Playful Structures

Traditional meetings create pressure; playful formats reduce cognitive load.

Design implication: gamification as behavioural catalyst.

04 — Design Strategy

Rather than relying on traditional meeting formats, the project introduces gamification to reduce participation barriers and encourage collaborative engagement.

AI-Driven Scenarios

AI cards translate abstract technologies into relatable everyday scenarios.

Phygital Sync

NFC-enabled pieces connect the physical game board with an online platform.

Neutral Debate

AI acts as a neutral third-party facilitator, analysing pros and cons.

05 — The Workflow

Guiding users from personal reflection to collective decision-making.

01

Empathy

Participants start from personal lived experiences and everyday pain points.

02

Ideation

Policy cards and technology cards help translate individual needs into visions.

03

Consensus

AI-assisted debating and anonymous voting filter noise and surface priorities.

04

Output

The system generates structured community planning proposals based on input.

06 — Iteration: From Physical to Phygital

Feedback Loop

Early workshops revealed concerns about group influence affecting voting outcomes.

The Pivot

A mobile application module was introduced to enable remote participation and reduce social pressure. A data dashboard visualises community sentiment as a “public opinion heatmap.”
